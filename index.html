01 The ProblemThe System Works Against People Who Are Ready to Act

Mental health resources exist. Therapy is more normalised than it has ever been, especially among adults aged 18 to 35. And yet, young adults who recognise they need help still struggle to get to a first appointment. The problem isn't awareness of mental health as a concept. It's that the path from recognising a need to sitting in a therapist's office is fragmented, confusing, and full of friction.

We wanted to understand what happens between the moment someone decides they need help and the moment they actually schedule an appointment. Where do people get stuck? What pushes them forward? And what could a well-placed intervention do to shorten that gap?

Research Questions: How can access to mental health services be increased for students and young professionals? What barriers prevent them from accessing mental health services?

02 The Research9 Interviews, One Recurring Story

We conducted 9 semi-structured interviews over Zoom with help-seekers aged 18 to 35. Each session lasted about 45 minutes and covered five topic areas: current stress and mental health, the decision to seek help, the process of finding a provider, social and cultural influences, and trust in the therapeutic relationship.

Our original plan included both help-seekers and non-help-seekers. Our one non-help-seeker participant was a no-show, so our findings are weighted entirely toward people who have already navigated the system. That's a real limitation, and it's worth noting that recruiting someone who hadn't sought help was harder than recruiting those who had. Whether that says something about our team's networks, our generation, or a broader cultural shift toward normalised help-seeking is an open question.

What we found was consistent across nearly all 9 sessions. Participants described a four-stage journey, and the barriers shifted at each stage but compounded as they went.

03 Emerging ThemesFive Patterns Across Nine Sessions

After coding and analysing all 9 transcripts, five themes emerged consistently. Each maps to a different part of the help-seeking journey and reveals a specific type of barrier.

1. The Crisis Threshold

Participants normalised their struggles until a breaking point forced action. 8 of 9 described functioning through distress before reaching a moment where the cost of staying away from help became undeniable.

2. Peer Influence as Catalyst and Permission

Peers shaped both the timing and the comfort level of help-seeking. For some, a friend's experience was the direct trigger. For others, peers didn't push the decision but made it feel acceptable.

3. The Navigation Phase

Even after deciding to seek help, participants faced outdated provider lists, confusing insurance routing, no central starting point, and scheduling conflicts with business hours.

4. Competing Norms Across Social Contexts

Therapy was normalised among friends but not in workplaces, families, or cultural communities. Participants managed what they shared based on context.

5. Trust Requires Authentic Connection

Every participant had specific trust criteria for providers. Identity fit, communication style, and openness were dealbreakers. A poor first match could end the process entirely.

I sat in my driveway and got super emotional. I was just like, something is wrong.

04 Hypothesis Check-InWhat We Predicted vs. What We Found

We entered the research with three hypotheses. By the end of the study, only one held up as written.

Not Supported

H1: Information Overload

We predicted too much information would overwhelm people. The opposite was true: too little useful information, scattered across too many places. We reframed this as information inaccessibility.

Partially Supported

H2: Perceived Public Stigma

Stigma wasn't a barrier among peers. It showed up in families, workplaces, and cultural backgrounds. Context-dependent, not universal.

Strongly Supported

H3: System Complexity

The most consistent barrier. Insurance limits, provider discovery friction, scheduling conflicts, and cost created delays at the exact moment people were ready to act.

05 Behavioural ArchetypeThe High-Functioning Holdout

Our research produced one behavioural archetype that described 8 of our 9 participants. We called them The High-Functioning Holdout: a young adult who is struggling quietly, performing well enough on the outside that the need for help doesn't feel "real enough" to act on. They delay seeking help not because they don't believe in therapy, but because their coping strategies are still working, until they aren't.

The trigger to seek help is rarely a plan. It's a moment of pain where the cost of avoiding help becomes greater than the fear of seeking it. EP008 broke down in their driveway. FR003 went on an international trip and felt nothing. SP007 noticed the anxiety kept cycling no matter what stressor she cleared.

KC004 represented an alternate pathway. With a pre-existing mental health advocacy background, they experienced minimal deliberation delay. But even KC004 followed the same internal logic: recognise distress, deliberate, research, schedule.

As long as I'm functioning and working and getting stuff done, I don't need a therapist. But to see what my friend went through kind of pushed me to just give a therapist a try.

Help-Seeker, Session RM001

06 Design ConceptMindWell: A Connected Ecosystem

The problem with mental health access is not one barrier. It is a chain of them, and they compound. Someone who finally decides to seek help still has to figure out insurance, find a provider they trust, and schedule around a workday. That chain is where people drop off.

Instead of building one tool that addresses one barrier, we designed a connected set of tools that covers the full chain. Each component is tied to a specific decision lever from our research.

Workshop + Campaign

90-minute educational workshop and social media storytelling. Makes distress feel personally applicable before someone hits a crisis.

Self-Assessment Tool

Non-clinical questionnaire that asks whether coping strategies are still working. Signals when the threshold may be approaching.

Provider Directory

Filterable by specialty, insurance, and identity fit. Puts the information participants were searching for in one place.

Direct Scheduling

Async intake outside business hours. Transparent costs. Reduces friction at the moment of highest resolve.

Decision Levers: Each component targets a specific behavioural lever: coping depletion cue (self-assessment), social proof (workshop and marketing), trust (identity fit filters), and friction reduction (scheduling integration).

07 ReflectionWhat I Took Away from This Project

This project confirmed something I had been thinking about for a while. I get more energy from understanding why a design decision makes sense than from designing the screens themselves. The analysis work, going through transcripts, tagging themes, connecting participant stories to behavioural patterns, was where I was most engaged. It's pushing me toward UX research as a career direction.

The biggest lesson was about pacing. Our lowest score came from presenting Emerging Themes based on two interviews when we needed nine. The data wasn't there yet, and it showed. Every submission after that improved because we had the evidence to back up what we were saying. The work wasn't better because we got smarter. It was better because we had the data.

I'd also communicate more proactively in future projects. There were moments during the quarter where life events pulled me away from team coordination, and I waited too long to flag that. In a remote team especially, silence gets interpreted as disengagement. Saying "I'm going to be less available this week" early is better than going quiet and trying to catch up after.

Studying behavioural science in a UX context changed how I think about design problems. The connection between research findings and design decisions doesn't have to be vague. If you can name the behavioural lever you're targeting, you can defend every design choice with evidence. That's the approach I want to take forward.
